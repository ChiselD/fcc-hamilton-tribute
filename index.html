Margaret Hamilton

One small step for man – one giant project for womankind.

Margaret Hamilton standing next to the code she wrote (photo courtesy of Wired)


  • Hamilton was the lead developer in writing the code that took the Apollo 11 astronauts to the moon
  • When she was hired as a programmer at MIT, she was only 24 and had an undergrad degree in math
  • She would often bring her young daughter Lauren to work with her
  • "Programming" at the time consisted of punching holes in cards and then threading wire either through or around them
  • Important concepts Hamilton came up with include asynchronous software, human-in-the-loop decision capability, and priority scheduling
  • Hamilton is also credited with coining the term "software engineering"
  • She still lives in Cambridge today, and has founded and led multiple software companies

"What they used to do when you came into this organization as a beginner, was to assign you this program which nobody was able to ever figure out or get to run. When I was the beginner they gave it to me as well. And what had happened was it was tricky programming, and the person who wrote it took delight in the fact that all of his comments were in Greek and Latin. So I was assigned this program and I actually got it to work. It even printed out its answers in Latin and Greek. I was the first one to get it to work."

– Margaret Hamilton, remembering her early days working at MIT
